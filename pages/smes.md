# 04 April, 2026

I think there are different ways to approach the subject of Small and Medium Enterprise (SMEs), also officially classified as MSMEs (Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises) in India. The first is to take a full blown management approach where I just look at SMEs from a management perspective - this primarily involves reading papers, books and articles from management academia - and this can be generic or specific to one or more countries. Another route that can be taken is the SMEs-policy route here you see SMEs as an organism operating under a particular SME policy of a nation - eg: India. Looking at SMEs in India and the policy revolving around it - this seems to give a better picture of how SMEs operate - and it tends to give way more specifics, stuff that can be generalized for a particular nation or a geography. Another potential advantage of the second over first method is that it considers an SME not in vaccum but in a reality of policy and other factors - the academic research is geared more in that direction. Because of these preliminary reasons, I would like to study SMEs in the Indian context in detail - from both perspectives. One basic imagery that came to my mind is the application of SOR theoretical framework to view an SME. The SME can be viewed as an organism with its own Strategy, management, organization, operations and so on, but operating in a clear external environment - political and regulatory reality, competition, social and environmental factors along with obvious technological factors and changes (PESTEL). The SME needs to be studied as an organism - the internals of it as well as how it operates in an environment - which probably can be seen as a bunch of stimuli and the SME/organism reacting/responding to it and operating in certain external conditions. In order to do this, I have been looking at SMEs (or MSMEs to be precise) in the Indian context, and the external conditions in particular - looking at papers on policy, industry examples and cases, how certain SMEs react to certain external changes (any one or more of the PESTEL factors - P might stand for political/policy in the case of SMEs I feel). So this is the direction I will go in. What is the end goal of all this? Strengthen the SME/organism from inside, make it more robust by designing better management principles, better business strategy and so on keeping all external and internal factors in mind as well as thinking of good policies to help SMEs grow - and the very important intersection - on how SMEs should/will operate under a certain policy/external condition - this is an intersection of management and policy.

How do you eventually ensure small businesses (SMEs) do well? Two ways: One at the internal level and other external. The first is through better management practices, better strategy and so on whereas the latter is with the help of policy. As far as I am concerned, I will be acting like a consultant to SMEs in the former way, I will be acting as a consultant to the government or such bodies in the latter way. The latter (policy way) works at a macro-level where as the former (management-strategy) is more micro and granular, with particulars of an industry-company-geography and such specifics. The former is micro but can probably be implemented at scale for many organizations with small customizations specific to industry/company. At this point, I need to decide which method I need to take up to study small businesses - I want to take the former way - the management-business-strategy way over the policy way.

Before moving forward, I would like to share a vague idea to view small businesses, from a theoretical/academic standpoint that can potentially help in making them better. External factors tend to have a greater effect on small businesses than big ones, and these small firms are not in control of them. So the external environment dictates / shapes good part of their business strategy. I think the small business can be seen as an organism with a certain core but with ideally high ability to respond/react to external stimuli. Theoretically, this fits with the SOR (Stimulus - Organism - Response) management framework. Studies focusing on small business navigating policy changes can be founded in this framework. From what I understand, the SOR framework is generally applied to understand consumer behavior where the organism is typically a human who responds to a stimulus (eg: you want to study impulse buying tendencies in a quick-commerce application). I have not seen the SOR framework being applied in the context of small businesses, it could be worth the try because it brings together business strategy and policy/environment.

# Risks, Challenges and Uncertainty in Small Businesses

I will be listing resources related to risks, challenges and uncertainty in small businesses - goal is to study them well and then see how to manage them 
and ensure the business stays alive. I think every business has (or ideally should have) predictable states to go to on executing a particular action - there can be many number of states, but we need to know them all - so essentially we have a neat state machine for a given business - at what level does this state machine exist? At tactical decision level or at strategy level? I think it is better to have a state machine at strategy level - that way we can focus on the big decisions (and not operational decisions). Eg: We are in a particular state, a strategy is made by the owner-manager and it is executed - the strategy works and we go to a better state (whatever it means), the strategy fails and we go back to a worse state (or worst state - point of no return is bankruptcy), or you maintain status quo (this hardly happens, even if the business does not move forward, the business would have lost money for sure). So I think before going to understand risks, challenges and uncertainty, we need to have a broad picture, a state machine for a typical small business that can help us bring more predictability into the picture. Or we will make one with time using current literature and new knowledge and write about it.

The first paper that I came across that even has something close to a state machine is that paper which describes the [5 stages of small business growth](https://www.hayekcollege.com/sites/default/files/ebooks/The_Five_Stages_Of_Small_Business_Growth.pdf). This paper presents a more useful, complex model of a model proposed in 1968 in this paper (https://ils.unc.edu/daniel/131/cco4/Greiner.pdf) that describes the 5 stages of growth of a generic business - we start here.

I want to model a typical small business as a state machine - let its finances denote a particular state machine, let its operations be denoted with another state machine - every decision (that is big enough) is how the business goes from one state to another - and the next state is either a better state (if that decision/strategy works), it can be a worse state (if the decision/strategy fails, the worst state being the state of bankruptcy from where there is no recovery) or maintain status quo (in the operational state machine but go to worse state in the financial state machine because we lost precious cash executing that strategy). I think an elaborate set of state machines to describe a particular small business - would give lot of predictability in handling risks, challenges and uncertainty. So my point is that - I want to first model a small business as a bunch of state machines and then look at the rest later. If there are any papers, reports etc., related to this, I want you to curate.

Two papers that gave somewhat of a structure for organizational development and growth are "Evolution and Revolution as Organizations Grow" by Greiner (1968) and "The Give Stages of Small Business Growth" (1983). The first was written for general businesses in mind (so mainlybig businesses) and the second is the thoughtful modification of the first - catering to small businesses. I re-read both papers, the first is not exactly useful for my endevour of creating state machines representing small businesses, but the second one is a good start. The paper actually gives a good state machine (Exhibit 4) on the organizational growth of small businesses. The Exhibit looks like a flowchart but it is actually a state machine - where there are end states, you go from one state to another and so on. This is a state machine that is very high-level - which can be used by us to evaluate organizations once in every couple of years to check which stage of growth the small business is in. Although this is good, this is not what I had in mind - this state machine does not help owner-managers execute short-term strategy, this is a very long-term view. What I have in mind is a state machine that probably is customized to the small business under lens - construct a state machine for it capturing the following information: The state machine I want operates at a lower-level than Exhibit-4. To reiterate, Exhibit-4 operates at a very high-level, it does not help in short-term strategy and decision-making for the owner-manager. At the same time, I don't want it to capture low-level strategy, planning and operational details - that is too granular and again does not help in short-term decision-making. We need something in the middle. Consider the following example:

1. Consider a tender-coconut business running at the corner of a landmark location - the business is simple, the owner himself takes care of day-to-day operations - which is cutting the tender coconut to customers - a business would need two state machines - one that captures the financial state of the business, another that captures the strategy-operational state of the business, and obviously, the financial state largely determines the strategy-operational state of the business (lets say the business is cash negative, the owner is essentially stuck at a particular state in the operational state machine and would need more capital to get out of it - basically more money to buy those tender coconuts and re-start the business).
2. Now the business wants to improve their revenues. The owner comes up with the strategy of selliing bottled tender coconut water (1L at 200Rs or something like that). This is a revenue strategy that the owner wants to execute. We have one new end state - that is the new state that is achieved after successfully operationalizing this strategy OR the business remains in the same state if the strategy fails - but its financial state definitely worsen in that case because of the failed strategy.

Alright, now that I think about it, the idea of state machine for short-term strategies do not seem to be viable - because to improve revenues, I can come up with a 100 ideas and one of them will be executed. All I was looking for was a predictable framework for a given business that can be used as a foundation to cover risks/challenges/uncertainty. But I think it is alright. I will go back to reading about risks/challenges/uncertainty in small businesses.
